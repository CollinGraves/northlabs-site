The Problem

Three gaps. No one bridges all of them.

Capital is pouring into physical-world industries. But the firms evaluating these deals aren't equipped for what technology looks like outside of a browser window.

The Generalist Gap

Your tech diligence provider runs the same playbook on a construction platform that they'd run on a fintech app.

Generalist diligence providers can tell you if the code is well-written. They can't tell you if the product actually solves the problems contractors face in the field - whether the mobile app works without cell service on a job site, whether the estimating logic accounts for regional labor rates, or whether the payment flow makes sense for how trades businesses actually get paid.

The Industry Gap

Industry consultancies know the market. They can't tell you if the technology behind the product is real.

Industry-focused advisors understand the commercial landscape - who the buyers are, how the market is structured, where the growth is. But when the target says their AI "predicts project delays" or their platform "automates compliance," these firms take the pitch deck at face value. Nobody on their team can open the hood and tell you what's actually under it.

The Operating Company Gap

Your deal model assumes technology-driven margin improvement. Who validates that?

When you acquire a $300M mechanical contractor, the value creation plan says technology will drive efficiency gains. But who evaluates whether that company can actually adopt new systems? What their current technology looks like? What it will cost and how long it will take to get them where they need to be?

Trades & Field Service

Rewriting the deal model for a field service platform by finding what generalist diligence confirmed but didn't understand

A PE firm's prior generalist tech DD provider had rated this platform favorably on standard metrics. North Labs found the five domain-specific issues that would determine whether the deal thesis actually worked.

Target: Field service management platform serving residential and commercial HVAC, plumbing, and electrical contractors. ~$45M ARR, 6,800+ contractor customers, 185,000 field technicians on the mobile app monthly.
$8-14M

5-6 pts

Actual NRR (Stable Base)

Flat-rate pricing engine was the real moat - 48,000+ line items with regional labor rates and supplier feeds. 18-22% average ticket increase. 3-4 year replication barrier.

Offline sync architecture was fragile: last-write-wins conflict resolution works for single technicians, breaks for the multi-crew commercial expansion the deal model assumed

Embedded fintech undermonetized by 40-60% - 1.1% blended take rate vs. 1.8-2.5% for comparable platforms. Processor renegotiation + financing products = largest value creation opportunity

AI scheduling was pre-production - 23% dispatcher adoption. Model optimized for drive time but ignored skill match, equipment requirements, and cascading delays

What a generalist would have missed

The 14% gross churn headline was actually 8-9% competitive churn plus seasonal contractor patterns. The offline architecture couldn't survive the commercial expansion plan. And the AI scheduling model would fail in the field because it didn't know what a dispatcher actually manages.

Industrial & Manufacturing

Reframing a $200M+ industrial AI acquisition from "buy the models" to "protect the data"

A prior diligence provider gave this platform a clean bill of health. North Labs found three deal-level risks they missed entirely - and a data monetization opportunity that wasn't in the deal model.

Target: Predictive maintenance and asset intelligence platform for heavy manufacturing, energy, and industrial facilities. ~$38M ARR, 1.2M connected sensors, 14 billion data points ingested daily across 340+ facility deployments.

Sensor data pipeline was the real asset - 17 industrial protocols, 18-24 months ahead of competitors. Reframed the acquisition from "AI company" to "data infrastructure company"

ML models overstated production accuracy: 92% on curated test data, 34% false positive rate in the field. Vibration-based models applied to equipment where vibration is irrelevant

OT security exposure invisible to prior IT-focused assessment - gateways with direct SCADA/DCS network adjacency in 38% of deployments. Zero IEC 62443 alignment

$6-12M data monetization opportunity the deal model hadn't sized - OEM benchmarking, insurance underwriting data licensing, energy optimization analytics

What a generalist would have missed

Predictive models trained on vibration data produce noise on electrical switchgear. The OT network boundary was the real security risk - not the cloud. The most valuable thing in the company was the data pipeline, not the AI.
